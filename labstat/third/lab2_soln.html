Statistics for laboratory scientists

Solutions for lab 2

  1. Concerning the distribution of the observed allele frequencies in the initial population, how do they vary for different sample sizes and different generating allele frequencies?

    The distribution of the observed allele frequencies is more tight (smaller standard deviation) in the case of a larger initial population, and is centered around the generating allele frequency.

  2. Concerning the trace of the observed allele frequencies across generation, how is the case of 100 couples different than the case of 25 couples, in terms of the fluctuation in the observed allele frequency across time/generation?

    The observed allele frequencies across generations vary more wildly in the case of a smaller population (and more slowly in the case of a larger population).

  3. Concerning the investigation of allele fixation:

    1. What proportion of the time was the allele frequency fixed at 100%?

      Using code like mean(res[,1]), one finds that it is fixed at 100% approximately 20% of the time. Of course, this will vary according to the particular 100 replicates we observed.

    2. What was the average and SD of the generation time at which fixation occurred?

      Using code like mean(res[,2]) and sd(res[,2]), one finds that the time of allele fixation has mean about 180. and SD about 180.

  4. Explain why the command pbinom( qbinom(0.75,120,0.512), 120, 0.512) doesn't return exactly 0.75.

    Because of the discreteness of the binomial distribution, there is no one number that is exactly the 75th percentile. If X ~ binomial(n=120, p=0.512), Pr(X <= 64) = 71% and Pr(X <= 65) = 77%. The 75th percentile is defined to be the smallest number q such that Pr(X <= q) >= 75%.

  5. If 10% of rats in Baltimore are infected with a particular virus, and I obtain a random sample of 100 rats, what is the chance that at least 10 of them are infected?

    Let X ~ binomial(n=100, p=0.1). We seek Pr(X >= 10) = 1 - Pr(X <= 9) = 1 - pbinom(9, 100, 0.1) = 55%.

  6. Use the function ppois to find the probability that a Poisson random variable with mean 0.5 will exceed (i.e., is strictly greater than) 1. Give both the answer and the R code.

    Let X ~ Poisson(mean=0.5). We seek Pr(X > 1) = 1 - Pr(X <= 1) = 1 - ppois(1, 0.5) = 9%.

  7. Suppose the heights of adult men in the US follow a normal distribution with mean 69 in and SD 3 in. If I pick a random adult male, what is the chance that he will be over 67 inches? over 72 inches? Give both the answers and the R code.

    Let X ~ normal(mean=69, sd=3).

    Pr(X > 67) = 1 - pnorm(67, 69, 3) = 75%.

    Pr(X > 72) = 1 - pnorm(72, 69, 3) = 16%.
